# Debt Snowball vs Debt Avalanche: Which Actually Works

If you're staring at three or four different debts trying to decide what to pay off first, you've probably already run into the debt snowball vs avalanche debate. Both methods have loud supporters. What most articles won't tell you is that picking the wrong one for *your* specific numbers can cost you real money, or real time — not just a feeling of "did I choose right."

Most advice on this topic stops at "just pick whichever keeps you motivated." That's not wrong, exactly, but it skips the part that actually matters: these two methods only diverge in a meaningful way when your debts have very different interest rates. If your rates are close together, the method barely matters. If one of your balances is sitting at a much higher rate than the rest, the method you pick can change how much interest you pay before you're done.

## What is the Debt Snowball Method?

The debt snowball method has you pay the minimum on every debt, then put any extra money toward the debt with the smallest balance first — regardless of its interest rate. Once that debt is paid off, you roll its payment into the next-smallest balance, and so on.

## What is the Debt Avalanche Method?

The debt avalanche method also has you pay the minimum on every debt, but the extra money goes toward the debt with the highest interest rate first — regardless of its balance size. Once that debt is paid off, you move to the next-highest rate.

## A Real Example, Worked Out By Hand

Say you have three debts:

* **Medical bill:** $1,200 balance, 10% interest, $50 minimum payment
* **Credit card:** $4,000 balance, 24% interest, $120 minimum payment
* **Car loan:** $6,000 balance, 6% interest, $180 minimum payment

You have $500 a month to put toward debt. Minimum payments total $350, leaving $150 extra.

**With the snowball method**, the extra $150 goes to the medical bill first (smallest balance). You'd clear that in a few months, then roll the freed-up minimum into the credit card, and finally the car loan.

**With the avalanche method**, the extra $150 goes to the credit card first (highest rate, at 24% — well above the roughly 21% average credit card rate reported by the Federal Reserve in 2026). That's the balance quietly costing you the most every month it sits untouched, so clearing it first cuts the most interest before it can pile up further.

In this example, the avalanche method saves more in total interest, because the credit card's 24% rate is so far above the other two. If all three debts had been sitting closer to the same rate, the two methods would have landed much closer together in total cost — and the choice would really come down to which one keeps you paying consistently.

## Which One Is Actually Right For You

* **Choose snowball if** you've stalled out before and need an early win to stay consistent. Paying off the smallest balance first gives you a visible result fast, even if it's not the mathematically cheapest path.
* **Choose avalanche if** you're already consistent with payments and want the lowest total interest paid, and you're comfortable not seeing a balance hit zero for a while.

Neither answer is more "correct" in general — it depends on your balances, your rates, and which one you'll actually stick with for the next several months.

## The Part That's Easy to Miss

The ranking above isn't fixed. Every time you make a payment, get charged interest, or pay down a balance, the "correct" next target can shift — especially with the avalanche method, where a balance's rate matters more than what you've already paid toward it.
